About Me

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Statistics at Stanford University, where I am fortunate to be advised by Professors John Duchi and Scott Linderman. My research is focused on building reliable and trustworthy artificial intelligence systems with applications in Neuroscience. I develop methods to make machine learning models robust to shifts in distribution and able to accurately relate their own uncertainty. The ultimate goal of my work is to apply these principles to Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) to help patients regain abilities like speech. My path to this field is interdisciplinary; I hold a B.A. in Physics from Brown University and an M.S. in Statistics from Stanford. Previously, I have conducted research in computational physics and molecular dynamics , and worked as a Cyber Defense Technology Specialist at Darktrace, applying Bayesian statistics to network security. Outside of research, I enjoy hockey, triathlons, and high-pointing. I also created a Bayesian model to predict pitcher performance for my fantasy baseball league, winning three of the past four seasons.

I am always happy to chat about research, statistics, or anything else. If you are ever around Stanford, I'd love to grab a coffee or hit a couple rallies on the tennis courts.
